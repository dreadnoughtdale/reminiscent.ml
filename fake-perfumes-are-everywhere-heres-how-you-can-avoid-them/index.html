Fake Perfumes Are Everywhere! Here’s How You Can Avoid Them

May 24, 2020May 24, 2020 Dale Bartolome Blog

The audacity of these sellers to even call fake perfumes as “Authentic US Tester Perfume” is so infuriating. These rogues may have already sold us some, only for us to find out we’ve been sold a fugazi; or worse, not even knowing they were fake and were priced on par with the real ones you can buy on outlet stores and malls. No matter what we’ve been through, we can all agree to a single common goal: THIS MUST BE STOPPED!

These types of markets exist due to demand. If we put an end to the driving force of their business, they will all be gone–sooner or later. The first step towards this is to prevent people from buying from them. To do this, we must let people know about the dangers of buying fake perfumes and the things to look for to identify if a certain fragrance is real or fake.

These will be discussed in this blog. Be sure to share this around once you’ve finished reading.

Here are the things you should look for to distinguish real and fake perfumes:

Declaration

This is the first thing you’re supposed to see in a product posting/ad. Based on observations, sellers of fake perfumes often use these words. So if you spot these, you know it’s fake:

  • Authentic US/Dubai/Singapore Tester
  • Class A/OEM
  • Original Tester
  • Overruns

There are lots more to list but at least you get the point.

The easiest way to know, however, is to ask the seller. If you ask “is this original/authentic?” and their response is anything else other than a simple yes, then it’s fake.

Although, testers do exist. These are original perfumes supposedly not for retail sales as these were packaged specifically for testing purposes. Agents use these to let customers know how the fragrance smells. Testers will never have the same packaging as the retail versions.

If you’re hesitant to buy testers but badly wanted to test a fragrance without having to go out of the comforts of your home, buy decants online instead. Reminiscent offers Decants of different scents you might be interested in. Be sure to check out our Shop.

Physical Appearance and Packaging

This part is quite tricky as some fake perfumes are terribly incredible in copying the qualities of the real perfume’s bottle and packaging. Still, you can spot a fake perfume even just by the aesthetics. Here are the things you should check:

  • Printed Text – Watch out for typographical errors. World-renown brands have the highest standards when it comes to quality control. There is no way they would get away with errors and wrong spellings in their packaging. If these mistakes are present, then the item is fake.
  • Batch Code – The codes on the bottom/back of the perfume bottle must match those on the box. You can request a picture of multiple boxes of their stocks. They’re not supposed to have the same batch codes. If they do, it’s a red flag. You can also search the batch code on Google + the keyword ‘fake?’ to know if this batch code is common among fake perfumes.
  • Close Up Pictures – Most fakes can’t copy the sprayer of the real ones. Fakes also seem to use either darker or lighter colors of ink in their packaging. Always search for pictures of real perfumes and compare it with what the seller posts or shows you. If you’re used to the upscale taste, it’s easy to know if it’s real or fake.

If something doesn’t feel right, trust your doubtful intuition.

  • Price Tag – Once this is present, it’s almost always legit. Look for the price tags of the top retail and department stores, both local (Rustan’s, Watsons, SM Dept. Store, etc.) and international (Sephora, Macy’s, Marshall’s, etc.) Some online sellers are selling mall pull-outs and I can confirm they’re authentic. Just be sure to look out for fraudsters who might have stolen photos from other sellers.
  • Paper Bags and Freebies – Fraudsters love keeping their items for sale as fancy and attractive as possible. Most of them even go to the extent of producing lame paper bags carrying the brand of the fake perfumes they sell. Real perfumes are usually packaged with the retailer’s paper bags. Most brands don’t have their paper bags. Official sample vials and cards are the most common freebies that come with authentic fragrances. If someone is offering you another big bottle as a freebie, be doubtful.

Pricing

This is the easiest way to spot fake perfumes. If they’re too good to be true, then they definitely aren’t true. Here are the pricing patters I have observed from sellers of fake perfumes:

  • Same Prices – From Niche fragrances to designers, from higher-tier designers to mid-range, from Chanel and Dior to Bvlgari and Versace. They’re all priced the same simply because they’re fake.
  • Bundle Price – Sellers of fake perfumes often use offers such as ‘buy one, take one’ and ‘buy one, take 50% off on your next bottle’ to attract customers. If they’re willing to give these offers, they were sourced cheaply.
  • Reseller Price – Some of these ‘business-minded’ sellers even offer special pricing for wholesalers, which is highly doubtful. We know that outlet and retail stores must meet certain standards in order to apply as resellers or consign products from a perfume brand’s distributor. If a seller has this kind of offer, it’s because the fragrances are fake.

Actual Smell

Nothing beats the reliability of actual testing in identifying real and fake perfumes. This is where testers come in handy. Ask an in-store sales agent to have you test the fragrance or ask for a sample. If you can’t go out, you can order samples and decants online. What’s Wrong With Buying Fake Perfumes, Anyway?

Most of us wouldn’t ever know how fake fragrances are made and where they even come from. We don’t know what they consist of and we can’t hold any company liable if these products bring hazards to our health.

Moreover, fake perfume sales and distribution are considered underground. They pay little to no taxes and they don’t provide legitimate jobs. They destroy the perfume market as they drive away and steal potential customers of official sellers.

It’s only right for us to do our part in keeping the perfume market healthy. Make everyone aware of this by sharing this blog around.
